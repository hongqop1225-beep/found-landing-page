The Dopamine Trap: your dating app was engineered to keep you single.

The burnout isn't a bug. It's the business model. Here's the neuroscience — and how we designed Found to do the opposite.

I want you to notice something small before we go anywhere.

Think about the last time you opened a dating app. Before you saw a single face, there was already a feeling in your body. A little flutter. A tightening. Your thumb was already moving before your eyes focused. That moment — the moment before the app loads — that's where all the damage is being done.

Not by you. To you.

Because what you were feeling wasn't excitement. It was dopamine anticipation. And every major dating app on the market is designed, from the ground up, to manufacture exactly that feeling as often as possible — because that feeling is what keeps you using the app, and using the app is what pays their bills.

Finding someone to love? That's a byproduct. And a rare one.

1. Your dating app is a slot machine.

I don't mean that as a metaphor. I mean it technically.

In 1957, the psychologist B.F. Skinner identified the most addictive reward schedule known to behavioral science: the variable ratio reinforcement schedule. It's when a reward comes unpredictably — sometimes after one action, sometimes after fifty — with no way to know which.

Slot machines use it. Gambling casinos are built on it. It is, by a significant margin, the single most powerful mechanism for getting an animal (human or otherwise) to keep performing a behavior long after any rational person would stop.

And it is the exact mechanism a swipe-based dating app runs on.

The neuroscience

"Dopamine rises twice as much in anticipation of a reward than when the reward actually arrives. Unpredictable rewards create the biggest spikes of all. Perfectly predictable rewards, eventually, stop triggering dopamine release altogether."

Read that again, because it's the secret everything else is built on. Your brain doesn't reward you for matching. Your brain rewards you for the uncertainty of whether you'll match. That's why the swipe feels better than the match. It's why you keep swiping after you've already matched with someone great. The anticipation is the drug.

This is not speculation. It's first-year neuroscience. And every UX designer at every major dating app company knows it.

2. The swipe is the perfect delivery system.

Think about what the physical gesture actually is. Your thumb hovers. You make a micro-decision. You flick. A face disappears. A new one appears. A tiny question mark hangs in the air: is this one the one?

That's not a matchmaking mechanic. That's a gambling mechanic dressed in romantic language.

Every swipe is a pull of a lever. Every match is a small win that trains your brain to pull again. Every non-match is an almost that makes the next pull feel more urgent. And when the rewards come randomly — which they do, by design — your brain learns to keep pulling, and pulling, and pulling, long after it's stopped feeling good.

This is why you can't "just log off." This is why people delete Tinder and redownload it a week later. This is why 79% of Gen Z say they're burned out on dating apps and yet can't stop using them.

Burnout isn't a personal failing. It's a neurological injury caused by a product deliberately engineered to exploit your reward system.

Tristan Harris, the former Google design ethicist who founded the Center for Humane Technology, has a name for this. He calls it the "race to the bottom of the brain stem" — the endless contest between tech companies to find shorter and shorter paths to your dopamine system, bypassing your rational mind entirely.

Dating apps didn't invent this race. But they run it harder than almost anyone.

3. They need you to stay single.

Here's the uncomfortable truth nobody at Match Group wants to say out loud:

The business model of every major dating app depends on you not finding someone.

If you meet the love of your life on Tinder tomorrow, Tinder loses a customer. If you stay single and anxious and scrolling for five years, Tinder makes five years of subscription revenue. Every product decision — the infinite feed, the gamified matches, the premium tiers, the paid super-likes, the notifications designed to pull you back in — flows from that incentive.

It's not evil. It's just math.

But it means the product in your pocket is, structurally, aligned against your outcome. You want a relationship. The app wants engagement. Those two goals are not the same. In the long run, they're opposites.

4. What dopamine-optimised dating does to your brain.

Here's what the last decade of research has shown happens to heavy dating app users:

  • Dopamine dysregulation. Your brain's baseline shifts. Ordinary life starts feeling flat. Coffee is less exciting. A friend's text is less exciting. Your own wins feel quieter. You've trained your reward system to expect slot-machine-level stimulation, and reality can't compete.
  • Decision fatigue. Each swipe is a micro-decision. By the time you've made 500 of them in a session, you're cognitively exhausted — and so are all the promising matches you skimmed past without noticing.
  • Performance erosion. When every interaction could be surveilled by a stranger scoring your profile in half a second, you stop being yourself. You start performing. And performance doesn't fall in love.
  • Attachment shortening. The knowledge that another match is one swipe away makes every current conversation feel disposable. Nobody tries. Nobody leans in. Because why would you?
  • Increased anxiety and depression. Multiple peer-reviewed studies link heavy dating app use with rising rates of depression, lowered self-esteem, and a measurable decline in mental health.

None of this is your fault. It's what happens when you hand your attention — every day, for years — to a system that was optimised for the wrong thing.

5. So we built the opposite.

When we started designing Found, we didn't start with features. We started with a question:

What would a dating app look like if it wanted you to close it?

Every design decision we made flowed from that question. Not "how do we keep people engaged?" — but "how do we help people actually find what they're looking for, as quickly and calmly as possible?"

Here's what that produced.

No swiping. At all.

Found has no profile feed. You will never swipe on Found, because swiping is the slot-machine mechanic, and we will not have a slot machine in an app that's supposed to help you find love. Full stop.

Set it and forget it.

When you first open Found, you tell us who you are, what you value, and what you're actually looking for. Then you close the app.

That's not a joke. That's the feature.

Behind the scenes, our AI matchmaker quietly does the work. It learns the shape of who you are, uses significant-change location to notice when a compatible person is physically nearby, and only ever interrupts you when it's genuinely worth it. One gentle notification. One real moment. Then back to your life.

You are not supposed to be on Found all day. You are supposed to be living, and Found is supposed to notice when your life is about to intersect with someone else's.

The AI does the searching. You do the meeting.

There's no filter to tune. No feed to browse. No inbox to maintain. Your job is to exist — to go to the coffee shop, to catch the ferry, to sit at the beach on Saturday morning. Our job is to notice when the right person is doing the same thing nearby.

It might take days. It might take weeks. That's the point. Real connection isn't fast. And anything that pretends to be is lying to you.

The design itself is a commitment.

You might have noticed that Found looks quiet. Cream background. Almost no colour. Soft serifs. No red badges. No pings. No gamification. No streaks. No "you have 3 new likes" explosion on the home screen.

That's not just aesthetic. It's neurological.

Every visual element in a modern app is a lever that can be pulled to activate your reward system. Bright colours, variable badges, animated celebrations, unlocked achievements — these are all dopamine triggers. We left them out. On purpose.

Found is designed to be low-stimulation, because your attention is the most precious thing you own, and we don't want to steal any of it.

6. Slower isn't a flaw. It's the whole idea.

Real connection in real life has never been fast. It takes time to notice someone. Time to catch their eye twice. Time to say hello. Time to let something build.

Dating apps broke that rhythm. They promised to speed it up — and ended up destroying it. A thousand matches in a week, and none of them real.

Found is our attempt to put the rhythm back. Fewer matches. Better ones. Over time. Because the thing you're looking for was never going to come from swiping harder. It was always going to come from being present in your own life while someone who fits you walks into it.

We just want to make sure you notice.

An app that wants you to close it.

If we do our job right, Found will be the least-used app on your phone. You'll open it once to set your preferences. Then you'll forget about it for weeks. Then one quiet Tuesday afternoon, while you're doing laundry or reading a book or walking along the beach, your phone will buzz softly — once — and the rest will be up to you.

That's the whole product. That's the whole philosophy.

Your brain has been under attack for a decade. Not by you. By products dressed as romance. We can't undo all of it. But we can refuse, on purpose, to run the same playbook.
