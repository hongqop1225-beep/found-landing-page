Why dating apps keep failing (and what Bumble's $8B mistake taught us).

Better discovery isn't enough. We didn't understand that until recently — and it changed how we built Found.

There's a story in tech right now that almost no one is talking about, and I can't stop thinking about it.

A seven-person startup called RealRoots just hit $782,000 in monthly revenue helping women make friends. Meanwhile Bumble — a publicly traded company once worth over eight billion dollars — has been trying to do the exact same thing for nine years, across three separate relaunches.

Per Bumble's most recent SEC filing, the BFF mode has generated zero dollars in revenue.

How?

The Matching Trap

Bumble owned the swipe and walked away. They assumed that if they gave women a better feed of potential friends — instead of potential dates — friendships would happen. Same interface, different goal.

They were wrong. Here's why.

RealRoots owns the part Bumble never touched: a facilitator picks the venue, runs the first meet-up, and keeps the same six women together for seven weeks until they're actually friends.

One product gave women more matches. The other gave them a friend group.

This has a name. Founders call it "The Matching Trap" — when a startup gives customers better discovery into their problem but doesn't reduce any of the actual work required to act on it.

Dating apps are stuck in the same trap

I kept reading this story and feeling a slow, uncomfortable recognition. Because if you squint, every dating app since 2012 has been running the exact same playbook Bumble BFF ran.

  • Tinder: here's 500 faces, you sort them.
  • Hinge: here's a slightly smaller feed, you still sort them.
  • Bumble: here's the same feed, but now you have to message first.

In every case, the app gave you more options for discovery. But the work of actually meeting someone — writing, waiting, second-guessing, coordinating, showing up — stayed 100% on you.

Is it any wonder everyone's burned out?

What the successful products have in common

RealRoots picks the venue. Picks the group. Runs the first meeting. Keeps the group committed for seven weeks.

That's not "better discovery." That's doing the work.

And it's exactly the move that's been missing from dating for a decade.

How Found is different

When we started building Found, we didn't have a framework for any of this. We just knew we were tired — tired of swiping, tired of scripted openers, tired of profiles that felt like auditions. So we asked ourselves a question:

What if the app did the hard part for us?

Not "here's a better feed." Here's the moment.

Found uses a quiet AI matchmaker that notices when someone compatible is actually near you in real life — and then helps you connect when the moment is right. No endless inbox. No writing essays to strangers. No 500-face decision fatigue.

The lesson I keep coming back to

Bumble didn't fail at BFF because women don't want friends. They failed because the problem isn't finding friends — it's doing the hundred small things between "match" and "friendship."

Dating is the same. The problem isn't finding a face. It's everything that comes after.

Found is our attempt to handle the everything-that-comes-after. To be the part of dating that every app has quietly asked you to do alone.
